Heritage

An Empire built on
Ochre Earth

Founded in the 11th century by Abu Bakr ibn Umar, Marrakech quickly grew into a powerful imperial capital. The city's signature red hue comes from the tabia (rammed earth) used to construct its imposing ramparts and buildings, designed to glow like embers in the setting sun.


Through the centuries, Saadian sultans and wealthy viziers embellished the city with lavish palaces, intricate zellige tilework, and sprawling gardens, creating an oasis of luxury at the edge of the harsh desert terrain.

Discover

Monuments & Magic

Behind every unassuming wooden door in Marrakech lies a potential palace. Here are the unmissable cornerstones of the city.

Jemaa el-Fnaa

The beating heart of Marrakech. By day, a vast market; by night, it transforms into a carnival of storytellers, musicians, acrobats, and sizzling food stalls.

Koutoubia Mosque

The spiritual compass of the city. Its magnificent 77-meter minaret is a masterpiece of Moorish architecture and inspired the Giralda in Seville.

Jardin Majorelle

A botanical sanctuary created by Jacques Majorelle and later restored by Yves Saint Laurent. Famous for its exotic plants and striking cobalt blue villa.

Bahia Palace

A 19th-century masterpiece of Moroccan architecture. Its endless courtyards, intricate stucco, and cedar ceilings were designed to capture the essence of Islamic and Moroccan style.

Culinary Tradition

The Legend of
The Tanjia

While Morocco is famous for the Tajine, Marrakech has its own unique culinary crown jewel: the Tanjia.

Unlike other dishes cooked by women at home, the Tanjia is traditionally prepared by men. Chunks of beef or lamb are stuffed into a clay urn with preserved lemons, garlic, cumin, and saffron. The urn is sealed and taken to the local hammam (public bathhouse), where it is buried in the hot ashes of the furnace to slow-cook for 12 to 18 hours until the meat melts in the mouth.
