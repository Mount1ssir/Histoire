Heritage

Life on the
Edge of the Sand

Merzouga itself is a small, quiet village on the edge of the Erg Chebbi dune sea. For centuries, the nomadic Amazigh (Berber) tribes navigated this harsh landscape using the stars and an intimate knowledge of hidden desert wells.


Today, the traditions of hospitality remain unbroken. Visitors are welcomed into the desert with sweet mint tea and traditional music. Life here is dictated by the extreme temperatures: the town sleeps during the searing midday sun and comes alive in the cool, magical hours of dawn and dusk.

"The desert does not belong to anyone. It is a vast ocean of sand that strips you of everything but yourself."
Discover

Monuments of Nature

In Merzouga, the landmarks are carved by the wind rather than by human hands.

Erg Chebbi

A spectacular sea of wind-blown sand dunes spanning an area of 50 kilometers. The sand constantly changes color as the sun moves, shifting from pale gold to burning orange and deep crimson at sunset.

Nomadic Desert Camps

Tucked deep inside the dunes, these traditional camps offer the ultimate Sahara experience. After arriving by camel, visitors spend the evening around a campfire listening to drums beneath the Milky Way.

Dayet Srji Salt Lake

Just west of Merzouga lies this seasonal salt lake. In the spring months, it magically fills with water, attracting flocks of pink flamingos and other migratory birds—a striking contrast against the dunes.

Khamlia Village

A small village near Merzouga famous for its population of Gnawa musicians. Descendants of sub-Saharan Africans, they perform deeply spiritual, rhythmic trance music using traditional castanets (krakebs).
