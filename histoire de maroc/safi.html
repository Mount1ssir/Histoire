History

A City
Shaped by Tides & Time

Long before European explorers charted the Atlantic, Safi was a prosperous Phoenician and Amazigh settlement, its natural harbour drawing traders from across the Mediterranean. In the 16th century, the Portuguese seized it and left behind the imposing Dar el Bahar fortress — the Castle of the Sea — still standing on the cliff's edge above the crashing waves.


The Saadian dynasty reclaimed the city in 1541, transforming it into a thriving commercial hub. Its medina — one of Morocco's oldest continuously inhabited — retains a labyrinthine character untouched by modern reconstruction, its lanes narrowing into cool, shaded corridors of whitewashed walls and carved cedar doors.

The Living Craft

The Colline des Potiers
Hill of the Potters

Safi's identity is inseparable from its clay. The Colline des Potiers — a hillside neighbourhood of open workshops and wood-fired kilns — has operated continuously since the 14th century. Visitors can watch masters shape raw earth on foot-powered wheels, then trace the finished pieces to their final resting places: market stalls, Moroccan riads, and tables across five continents.

Safi ceramics are recognisable by their bold palette — deep cobalt blues, earthy ochres, and forest greens — applied with geometric precision. Each workshop carries its own signature motifs, secrets passed down from father to son across countless generations.

The Coast

Beaches of Safi

Safi's Atlantic coastline stretches for kilometres of wild, sun-bleached shore. From city beaches alive with fishing boats to hidden cliffside coves, each spot has its own character.

01
Plage Safi

The main city beach, stretching just north of the old medina walls. A long sandy arc where local families gather at dusk, fishermen drag their nets ashore at dawn, and surfers ride the consistent Atlantic swell. Simple cafés serve mint tea and grilled sardines steps from the water.

02
Lalla Fatna

Probably the most celebrated beach near Safi. Sheltered by dramatic golden cliffs, Lalla Fatna is ideal for swimming and surfing alike. The waves roll in with regularity, and the sunsets here — framed by the clifftops — are among the finest on the Atlantic coast.

03
Cap Beddouza

Located roughly 30 km north of Safi, this wild stretch of golden sand unfolds at the foot of a dramatic promontory crowned by a white lighthouse. Uncrowded and largely untouched, the cape is a paradise for solitude seekers and photographers.

04
Kram Dif

A rugged cove beloved by locals and little known to passing travellers. Kram Dif's rocky shoreline creates natural tide pools alive with sea creatures, and the surrounding cliffs offer excellent vantage points over the Atlantic. Best visited at low tide.

05
Bedoza

A tranquil, less-visited beach with clear water and a wild, unspoiled setting. Bedoza draws those looking to escape the city's energy — a place for long walks along the shoreline and the rare pleasure of having an Atlantic beach almost entirely to yourself.

06
Walidiya

A peaceful stretch south of Safi where the coast softens and the pace slows. Walidiya's wide sands face due west, making it one of the best beaches in the region for watching the sun drop into the Atlantic. A favourite among local families for weekend outings and evening walks.

07
Souiria Lakdima

Located 36 km south of Safi, this expansive beach is considered the most active in the region. Consistent trade winds make it ideal for kitesurfing and windsurfing, while its fine sand and clear waters attract families and swimmers year-round.

08
Oualidia

Around 80 km north of Safi, Oualidia is one of Morocco's most beautiful coastal secrets. A sandbar separates the wild Atlantic from a calm lagoon perfect for swimming and kayaking. The surrounding oyster farms make it the finest spot on the coast to eat fresh shellfish by the water's edge.

On the Table

The Flavours
of the Atlantic

Safi's cuisine is shaped by the sea. With the world's largest sardine fleet at its doorstep, the city's tables are laden with the freshest Atlantic catch, prepared with Moroccan spices and charcoal smoke.

🐟
Grilled Sardines

Safi's flagship dish. Split, seasoned with chermoula, and grilled over open charcoal by the harbour. Best eaten hot, with fresh bread and harissa.

🦐
M'qila dial L'hout

A Safi classic — a generous pan of shrimp, calamari, and seasonal seafood sautéed in olive oil with garlic, saffron, and a secret blend of local spices. Rich, fragrant, and deeply satisfying.

🍪
Kaak de Safi

The beloved biscuit of Safi, known across Morocco. Lightly spiced with anise and sesame, baked golden and crisp — a staple of every Safi household and a souvenir worth bringing home.

Plan Your
Visit

📍
Getting There

Safi is 280 km south of Casablanca on the N1 coastal road. Buses depart daily from CTM stations in Casablanca and Marrakech. The drive along the Atlantic coast is spectacular.

🌤️
Best Season

Spring (March–May) and autumn (September–November) offer mild Atlantic weather. Surfers prefer November–March for the biggest swells at La Cathédrale.

⏱️
Recommended Stay

2–3 days to explore the pottery hill, fortress, medina, and coastline at a relaxed pace — and have time to commission a ceramic piece from the potters.

Traveller's
Tips

🏺
Buy Directly from the Potters

Walk uphill past the National Ceramics Museum to the working workshops. Pieces bought directly from artisans are a fraction of medina shop prices and carry a more personal story.

🌅
Watch the Sunset from the Ramparts

Climb to the upper terraces of Dar el Bahar in the late afternoon. The Atlantic turns gold, fishing boats return to harbour, and the call to prayer drifts from the medina below.

🍽️
Eat at the Harbour

The informal grills lining the fishing port serve the freshest sardines in Morocco, caught hours before they are placed on the coals in front of you.
